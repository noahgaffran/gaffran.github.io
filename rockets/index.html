QRET
I have been on QRET since 2018. After working on a succesful rocket in first year I became the aerodynamics lead, and designed the rocket seen at left, Now For Plan A, which flew to 25,500 ft at Mach 1.5+. I am now the CTO of the team and am working on a new rocket that will fly to above 30,000 ft and close to Mach 2. Additionally, if budget and time allow, a second rocket I have designed may be constructed - this one an attempt to beat the world "M" motor class altitude record of 45,500 ft, which would require speeds of nearly Mach 3.
UBC Rocket
While on internship in Vancouver, I joined the UBC rocket team. I worked on the airframe and internal structures of the "Whistler" rocket which is a liquid bipropellant rocket intended to reach space. Despite not being a UBC student, I was made subteam lead for a four-month period before my departure. I worked on thermal protection, intertank structures, fin optimisation, composites, and the prototyping of a sheet metal flush riveted nosecone. This rocket is BIG - the nosecone is almost my height.
Composites
One of my favourite areas of rocketry is structural composites. I have worked with QRET and UBC
CFD
I have used CFD to model rocket flight performance, and to iterate designs of fins, nose cones, and airframes, for aerodynamic performance, aerothermodynamic heating, and flutter.
Flight simulation
I developed a python-based rocket flight simulation program used to compare CFD-based aerodynamic performance to data output from off-the-shelf rocket simulation software.
Level 1 Certification High Power Rocket
Usually built from a kit, I decided to design my own rocket to get high-power certified by the Canadian Association of Rocketry. This rocket should fly to 1700 ft.
"Icarus" Sun-tracking rocket
This project is a multi disciplinary one and one of my first ventures into custom on-board electronics as well as active rocket control. The goal is to actively stabilize the rocket by using the position of the sun in the sky using photoresistors.
